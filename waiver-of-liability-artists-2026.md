## So what changed from Daniela's original draft?

Daniela sent a solid starting point. But it was built on top of old reference docs from a different event, with a Zaragoza-based arbitrator nobody at NCA has ever met, and it only covered grant recipients. Here's what we fixed:

**Who does this cover now?** Everyone. Every artist participating at Elsewhere — whether they got a grant or not. The bursary clause is now conditional ("where applicable"), so the same waiver works for funded and unfunded artists alike. One document, all artists, no confusion.

**Where do disputes go?** Huesca. The event happens in Huesca, the other Elsewhere contracts point to Huesca, so this one should too. Clean, consistent, no surprises.

**What happened to that arbitrator in Zaragoza?** Gone. Eduardo Esteban Jiménez was copy-pasted from old Nowhere documentation. He has zero connection to NCA, he's registered with the Zaragoza bar, and having one named person as your arbitrator is a single point of failure — what if he's unavailable, conflicted, or retired? International artists seeing an arbitrator hand-picked by the event organiser is also not a great look for trust.

**What replaced it?** Mediation first — 30 days of good faith, try to sort it out like adults. If that doesn't work, courts of Huesca. It's cheaper, faster, and doesn't depend on any single person. Standard approach for event-artist disputes across the EU.

**Why did "the Event" become "the Association"?** Because "the Event" is an activity — it's a thing that happens in July. NCA is the legal entity signing the contract. You can't sue an event. You sue the association that organised it. Cleaner legally, clearer for artists.

**Location got specific.** "In Spain" became "in the province of Huesca, Spain." Because telling an artist from Berlin that the event is "in Spain" is technically correct but practically useless, and it matters for governing law.

---

## Wait — are there things I should actually look at before signing off?

Yes. Four things. Three need a decision, one's already handled.

### The liability clause was a bit one-sided — is it still?

The original said NCA can't be held liable for *anything* related to the art piece. Full stop. That's a blanket waiver, and under Spanish civil law (Arts. 1101-1107 CC), a court can throw those out if NCA was actually negligent. If your scaffolding crew drops a beam on someone's sculpture, "not our problem" won't hold up.

**What I did:** Added a carve-out for gross negligence and wilful misconduct. The artist still takes responsibility for their own piece — that's the whole point — but NCA doesn't get a free pass if NCA itself does something reckless. This makes the clause actually enforceable instead of theoretically broad but legally fragile.

> If you disagree, tell me and I'll strip the carve-out back out. But I'd leave it in.

### Should insurance be mandatory for fire-breathing dragons and climbable towers?

Right now the clause says the artist "reserves the right" to get insurance. Which is a polite way of saying it's optional. For a painting, fine. For a propane-fueled flame sculpture or a 10-metre climbing structure? You probably want proof of liability insurance before that thing goes up.

Burning Man requires it. Nowhere has moved towards it for large installations. It's standard practice.

> **Your call:** Keep it optional across the board, or add a requirement for high-risk installations? If yes, I'll add a sentence.

### There's no GDPR clause and we're collecting personal data

The waiver asks for full name, ID document type and number, nationality, and home address. That's personal data under GDPR. NCA should either include a brief data processing notice in this document or reference NCA's privacy policy.

It's not a deal-breaker, but a Spanish data protection authority would raise an eyebrow if an association is collecting ID numbers without telling people what they're doing with them.

> **Your call:** Want me to add a data protection clause? Two paragraphs, nothing heavy.

### Which language version wins if there's a conflict?

Spanish takes precedence. That's standard for contracts governed by Spanish law — the courts in Huesca operate in Spanish, and if there's ever a dispute, the judge reads the Spanish version. Even though most artists will read the English column, the Spanish text is the one that matters legally.

> **No decision needed** — keeping this as-is. Just flagging so you know.
